1
To Open

Opening Hook

🎤 Leader Instruction Start with this question. Do not explain it yet. Let it sit in the room. Give your sons space to answer freely.
Ask Your Sons

"If someone walked into our home today and said — 'I can forgive every wrong thing you have ever done' — what would you think of that person?"

⏸ Pause — then follow with: "Now — what if that same person also said, 'I existed before your grandfather's grandfather was born'?"
🎤 Then say this: "That is exactly what one man said. In public. To people who knew their scriptures better than most of us ever will. And those people did not laugh at him. They picked up stones."

"A child who is not yet named is still someone's son. A name does not create the person. It simply reveals who was already there."

African Proverb — Opening Story

For centuries, Israel was waiting for someone. They had a name ready — Messiah. The Anointed One. The promised king. But when he came, he said something that shattered their categories entirely. He did not just wear the name they had prepared. He claimed to be the one who had been there before the name existed.

That is what this study is about. Not just who Jesus claimed to be — but why those claims demand a response from each of us.

2
Background

Setting the Scene

📖 Leader Instruction Read this aloud, or paraphrase naturally in your own voice. Keep it conversational.

To understand what Jesus said, you first need to understand the world he said it in.

Israel in the first century was a nation under occupation. Rome controlled everything — the roads, the taxes, the courts, and the sword. The Jewish people were humiliated. And they were waiting.

Their scriptures had promised a deliverer. A king from the line of David who would come and restore what had been broken. They called this figure the Messiah — from the Hebrew Mashiach, meaning "the Anointed One." The Greeks translated it as Christos. We say Christ.

Critical Point

In Jewish understanding, the Messiah was a man. Extraordinary — chosen by God — but still a human being. Not God himself walking among them.

God was in heaven, holy and untouchable. The Messiah was his appointed representative on earth. These were not casual opinions. They were deep, carefully held convictions shaped by centuries of scripture, suffering, and hope.

Nobody — not even his fiercest critics — was asking whether a man could be God himself. That question felt almost too extraordinary to ask. Until Jesus made it impossible to avoid.

💭
Discussion Pause

"If you had grown up your whole life believing God was in heaven and the Messiah was simply his chosen man on earth — how would you have reacted to someone claiming to be more than that?"

3
Key Concept

The Distinction That Matters

Before we look at what Jesus actually said, we need to settle something important. Not every claim Jesus made was a claim to be God.

Some were Messianic claims — and those are different.

Category The Messiah God Himself
Nature A chosen human being The Creator — eternal, uncreated
Role Restore Israel, rule in justice Sovereign over all things
Expectation Would arrive in history Had always existed
Authority Delegated by God Inherent — belonging to himself

A Jewish person in the first century could hear "Jesus is the Messiah" and disagree — but they would understand the claim. It fit within their framework.

But certain things Jesus said did not fit any framework they had. They went beyond Messianic expectation entirely. Those are the statements that caused the real crisis. Those are the four we are going to examine next.

💭
Think About It

"Can you think of a time someone was asked one question — but their answer revealed something much bigger than the question itself?"

4
Scripture Study

The Claims That Cannot Be Explained Away

📖 Leader Instruction In each claim below — pay attention to TWO things: what Jesus says, and how the people around him react. Their reaction is often the clearest evidence of what they understood him to mean.
Claim One · John 8:58

He Existed Before Time

The religious leaders are arguing with Jesus about his authority. They ask whether he thinks he is greater than Abraham — the founding father of their entire nation.

"Before Abraham was — I am."

John 8:58

Read that slowly. He does not say "I was." He says "I am" — present tense, about a past that predates all of human history.

Those two words — I Am — are not accidental. In Exodus 3:14, when Moses asks God his name, God replies: "I AM WHO I AM." Every Jewish person in that courtyard knew exactly what those words carried.

They picked up stones immediately. Not because he claimed to be old. Because he claimed to carry the name of God.

💬
Discussion Question

"Why do you think Jesus used those exact words rather than simply saying 'I existed before Abraham'?"

Claim Two · John 10:30

He Shared God's Nature

Short. Devastating. Impossible to soften.

"I and the Father are one."

John 10:30

The crowd's response removes all ambiguity. In verse 33 they say plainly — "You, a mere man, claim to be God." They were not misreading him. They understood precisely what he meant.

This was not a Messianic claim. No Jewish tradition ever taught that the Messiah and God were one. This was something else entirely.

💬
Discussion Question

"If Jesus had not meant to claim divinity here, what do you think he would have said to correct them?"

Claim Three · Mark 2:5–7

He Held Authority That Belonged Only to God

A paralysed man is lowered through a roof by his friends. The room is watching. Jesus looks at him and says:

"Son, your sins are forgiven."

Mark 2:5

The scribes sitting nearby are immediately troubled. Their objection is not about healing. It is about those four words. They say amongst themselves — "Who can forgive sins but God alone?"

They were theologically correct. In Jewish understanding, sin was ultimately an offence against God. Only God himself could absolve it. Jesus does not correct their logic. He simply validates his claim by healing the man — and lets the conclusion stand.

💬
Discussion Question

"The scribes were right that only God can forgive sins. What does Jesus's response tell us about how he understood his own authority?"

Claim Four · John 20:28

He Accepted Worship

After the resurrection, Thomas — who had doubted everything — sees Jesus and says:

"My Lord and my God."

John 20:28

He is not speaking about God in the distance. He is addressing Jesus directly. And Jesus receives it. No correction. No redirection. No "Thomas, be careful — worship only the Father."

Every devout Jewish teacher in history, when accidentally addressed with divine titles, would have recoiled immediately. Jesus does not recoil. His silence is his answer.

💬
Discussion Question

"Thomas had been a doubter. Why do you think his confession ended up being the most direct statement of Jesus's divinity in all four Gospels?"

Summary

These four moments are not Messianic claims. They sit in a different category altogether. Taken together, they form a portrait that the earliest followers of Jesus understood clearly — and that cost many of them everything.

5
Personal Response

The Moment of Decision

❤️ Leader Instruction Read this slowly and personally. This is the heart of the session. Do not rush through it.

There is a moment in every person's life when information becomes insufficient.

You can study the arguments. You can follow the logic. You can find the claims compelling and the evidence sound. But at some point, the question stops being academic and becomes entirely personal.

💡 C.S. Lewis — Liar, Lunatic, or Lord

C.S. Lewis — one of the sharpest minds of the twentieth century and a former atheist — argued that Jesus, given everything he claimed, left us only three possibilities.

❌ Liar
Knowingly deceiving people
😵 Lunatic
Genuinely deluded
✝ Lord
Exactly who he claimed to be

When you read the four claims we examined — the pre-existence, the unity with the Father, the forgiveness of sins, the acceptance of Thomas's worship — none of them feel like the words of a deceiver or a madman. They feel like the words of someone who knew exactly who he was.

✍ A Note for Dad — Your Personal Moment

This is the moment in the session where your own voice matters most. You do not need a script. Share briefly — when did the claims of Jesus move from something you knew intellectually to something you held personally? Your sons need to hear that journey in your own words, not in a curriculum. Even two or three honest sentences from a father carry more weight than an hour of teaching.

🙏
Ask Them — Quietly, Without Pressure

"Where are you with this? Not where do you think you should be — but where are you actually?"

6
To Close

Closing Reflection

📖 Leader Instruction Read this aloud. Slowly. Let each line breathe. This is not a lecture — it is a landing place.

There is an old rhythm to how truth arrives in Africa.

It does not always come loudly, announced with trumpets and proclamations. Sometimes it comes quietly — like dawn breaking over the Aberdares, unhurried, inevitable. You do not argue with the sunrise. You simply find yourself standing in light you cannot explain.

Jesus came the same way.

He did not hand the world a signed declaration. He spoke words that carried the weight of eternity. He forgave what only God could forgive. He received worship that only God deserved. He claimed a name that Moses had heard from a burning bush in the wilderness.

And then he waited.

He is still waiting.

Not impatiently. Not with a deadline. But with the quiet certainty of someone who knows that light, given enough time, finds every dark corner.

"Who do you say that I am?"

— Matthew 16:15

🙏 Close in Prayer Together

You do not need a written prayer. Speak as a father. Thank God for the gift of his Son. Ask for the courage — for yourself and for your boys — to follow the evidence wherever it leads, and to hold the answer not just in the mind but in the life.

A Final Word for You, Victor

The most powerful thing in that room will not be the arguments. It will not be C.S. Lewis or the Greek word egō eimi. It will be a father who has clearly decided — after years of life, work, and faith — that Jesus is exactly who he said he was. Your sons are watching that more than they are listening to the words.

Lead well. ✝
